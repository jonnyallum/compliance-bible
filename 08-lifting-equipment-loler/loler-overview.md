## Plain English Summary

The Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations 1998 require lifting equipment and lifting operations to be strong enough, stable, properly planned, supervised and thoroughly examined.

For FM, LOLER covers equipment such as passenger/goods lifts, hoists, lifting accessories, lifting beams, mobile lifting equipment and equipment used to lift people or loads.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Lifting failures can cause fatal injury, trapped passengers, falling loads, damaged assets and business interruption. In many buildings, lift compliance is also highly visible to users and auditors.

Facilities Managers must understand the difference between routine maintenance and statutory thorough examination. A service visit does not replace a LOLER thorough examination by a competent person.

## Who Has Duties

- Employers.
- People and companies who own, operate or control lifting equipment.
- Facilities Managers responsible for lifts or lifting equipment.
- Competent persons carrying out thorough examinations.
- Contractors planning or carrying out lifting operations.
- Employees using lifting accessories or lifting equipment.

## What the Law Requires

The core requirements include:

- lifting equipment must be strong, stable and suitable;
- lifting equipment used for lifting people must meet additional safeguards;
- lifting equipment and accessories must be visibly marked where appropriate;
- lifting operations must be properly planned by a competent person;
- lifting operations must be appropriately supervised and carried out safely;
- lifting equipment must be thoroughly examined at required intervals or under an examination scheme;
- defects must be reported and acted on.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- keep a lifting equipment and accessories register;
- identify which assets require LOLER thorough examination;
- schedule thorough examinations separately from maintenance;
- review competent person reports and close defects;
- remove unsafe lifting equipment from use;
- check passenger lift emergency communication and rescue arrangements;
- require lift contractors to provide reports promptly;
- ensure lifting plans and RAMS are in place for project lifts;
- verify contractor competence for lifting operations;
- maintain records for equipment used to lift people.

## Evidence to Keep

- Lifting equipment register.
- Thorough examination reports.
- Written scheme of examination where used.
- Lift maintenance records.
- Defect reports and remedial evidence.
- Lift emergency communication test records where relevant.
- Lifting plans.
- RAMS and permits for lifting operations.
- Competent person and contractor competence records.
- Records for lifting accessories, slings and attachments.

## Review Frequency

- Thorough examination: commonly every 6 months for equipment lifting people and lifting accessories, and every 12 months for other lifting equipment unless a written scheme sets a different interval.
- Maintenance: as specified by manufacturer, contractor and risk assessment.
- Lifting plans: before each lifting operation, proportionate to risk.
- Defects: review immediately and prevent use where serious defects are reported.
- Equipment register: update when assets or accessories are added, removed, modified or taken out of service.

## Red Flags

- Lift service records are treated as LOLER thorough examination records.
- Thorough examination reports are missing or overdue.
- Serious defects remain open with no evidence of action.
- Lifting accessories have no ID or inspection record.
- Project lifts take place without a lifting plan.
- Passenger lift alarm or communication failures are not escalated.
- No competent person appointed for examination.
- Equipment is used after a dangerous defect is reported.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I keep maintenance and statutory examination separate in my mind. Maintenance keeps equipment working; LOLER thorough examination independently confirms whether lifting equipment is safe to continue using.
