# Electricity at Work Regulations 1989

## Plain English Summary

The Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 require electrical systems and work activities to be designed, installed, maintained and operated so they do not give rise to danger.

For FM, the duty is broader than portable appliance testing. It covers fixed electrical installations, distribution boards, plant, temporary supplies, isolation, contractor competence and safe working near electrical systems.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Electrical failures can cause shock, burns, fire, plant outages and fatal incidents. FM teams often control maintenance access, emergency repairs, contractor works, portable equipment, plant rooms and temporary event supplies.

A Facilities Manager does not need to be an electrician, but must be able to prove that competent people manage electrical risks and that systems are maintained to prevent danger.

## Who Has Duties

- Employers.
- Dutyholders who control electrical systems or work activities.
- Facilities Managers commissioning or controlling electrical work.
- Competent electrical contractors and appointed persons.
- Employees using electrical equipment.
- Contractors using temporary supplies or bringing electrical equipment onto site.

## What the Law Requires

The core requirements include:

- electrical systems must be constructed and maintained to prevent danger;
- work activities must be carried out safely;
- equipment must be suitable for its environment and use;
- conductors must be protected against excess current;
- electrical equipment must be protected from adverse conditions;
- systems must have suitable isolation and switching arrangements;
- live working must be avoided unless justified and controlled;
- people must have the technical knowledge or experience needed to prevent danger.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- keep an electrical asset and distribution board register;
- ensure fixed wiring inspection and testing is completed by competent contractors;
- act on Electrical Installation Condition Report observations;
- maintain a portable equipment inspection/testing regime based on risk;
- control access to electrical cupboards and switch rooms;
- require permits and isolation evidence for higher-risk electrical work;
- check contractors provide competence evidence and RAMS;
- control temporary electrical supplies for events and projects;
- record emergency lighting, fire alarm and life-safety electrical dependencies separately;
- investigate repeated tripping, overheating, damaged sockets or unsafe leads.

## Evidence to Keep

- Electrical Installation Condition Reports.
- Remedial completion certificates.
- Portable appliance inspection/testing records where used.
- Electrical asset register.
- Distribution board schedules.
- Contractor competence records.
- RAMS and permits for electrical work.
- Isolation and lock-off records for higher-risk work.
- Thermal imaging reports where used.
- Temporary electrical supply sign-off.
- Incident and defect records.

## Review Frequency

- Fixed wiring inspection: at intervals set by competent assessment, commonly up to 5 years for many commercial premises, shorter where risk is higher.
- Portable equipment checks: risk-based, considering equipment type, environment and use.
- Electrical defects: review immediately and track to closure.
- Contractor competence: check before appointment and periodically.
- Temporary supplies: check before use and during longer events/projects.

## Red Flags

- EICR is expired or unsatisfactory observations remain open.
- Electrical cupboards used for storage.
- Repeated breaker trips without investigation.
- Damaged sockets, plugs or extension leads in use.
- Contractors working live without clear justification and controls.
- No evidence of competence for electrical contractors.
- Temporary event supplies installed without sign-off.
- Portable appliance testing treated as the only electrical safety control.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I manage electrical safety through competent contractors, current inspection evidence, controlled access, defect close-out and a risk-based approach to portable and temporary equipment.
