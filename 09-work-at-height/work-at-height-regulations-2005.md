## Plain English Summary

The Work at Height Regulations 2005 require work at height to be avoided where reasonably practicable. Where it cannot be avoided, it must be properly planned, supervised and carried out safely using suitable equipment and competent people.

For FM, work at height includes roof access, ladder use, MEWPs, scaffold, ceiling void access, gutter work, lighting replacement, signage, tree work, fragile surfaces and contractor maintenance.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Falls from height remain one of the most serious workplace risks. FM teams often arrange reactive roof repairs, gutter clearance, inspections, events, decorations and maintenance in live buildings.

The main FM failure is allowing routine tasks to proceed without asking whether height can be avoided, whether the right equipment is selected and whether fragile surfaces, edge protection and rescue arrangements have been considered.

## Who Has Duties

- Employers.
- People who control work at height, including Facilities Managers and building owners.
- Contractors planning or carrying out work at height.
- Supervisors coordinating the work.
- Employees using access equipment.
- Designers or project managers influencing access arrangements.

## What the Law Requires

The core requirements include:

- avoid work at height where reasonably practicable;
- prevent falls where work at height cannot be avoided;
- minimise distance and consequences of a fall where fall prevention is not possible;
- ensure work is properly planned, supervised and carried out safely;
- ensure people are competent;
- select suitable work equipment and prioritise collective protection over personal protection;
- manage fragile surfaces and falling objects;
- inspect work equipment and places of work where required;
- control weather and environmental conditions.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- challenge whether work at height can be avoided;
- maintain roof access rules and access permits where needed;
- keep roof plans showing fragile surfaces, edge risks and anchor points;
- require RAMS for contractor work at height;
- check competence for ladder, scaffold, MEWP and rope access tasks;
- ensure ladders are used only where risk and duration justify them;
- verify scaffold handover and inspection records;
- check MEWP rescue arrangements and exclusion zones;
- inspect fixed ladders, fall-arrest systems and eyebolts where present;
- stop work during unsafe weather or changing site conditions.

## Evidence to Keep

- Work at height risk assessments.
- RAMS and permits.
- Roof access permits and logs.
- Ladder/register inspection records.
- Scaffold handover and inspection records.
- MEWP operator competence evidence.
- Fall protection inspection records.
- Rescue plans where fall arrest or MEWPs are used.
- Contractor competence records.
- Photographs or plans of roof hazards.
- Incident, near-miss and defect records.

## Review Frequency

- Work at height risk assessments: before work starts and after changes, incidents or new hazards.
- Ladders and access equipment: pre-use checks plus formal inspections at risk-based intervals.
- Scaffold: inspect before first use, after events that may affect safety and at required intervals while in use.
- Fall protection systems: inspect at manufacturer/statutory/risk-based intervals.
- Roof access arrangements: review after building changes, contractor feedback, incidents or at least annually.

## Red Flags

- Ladders used for long-duration or high-risk tasks without justification.
- Roof work starts without fragile surface information.
- No edge protection or fall prevention where it is reasonably practicable.
- Fall arrest used without rescue arrangements.
- Scaffold has no handover or current inspection tag/record.
- Contractors working at height without RAMS.
- MEWP used without exclusion zone or trained operator evidence.
- Weather conditions ignored.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I use the work-at-height hierarchy first: avoid the height if possible, prevent a fall if not, and only then manage the consequences. I expect the equipment, competence and rescue arrangements to match the task risk.
