# Risk Assessment Framework

## Plain English Summary

Risk assessment is the practical process of identifying what could cause harm, deciding who may be affected, choosing proportionate controls and proving those controls are reviewed.

For Facilities Management, risk assessment must sit inside daily operations. It should guide maintenance priorities, contractor controls, inspections, permits, events, visitor safety decisions and escalation to senior management.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

FM teams control the physical workplace. Slips, trips, fire doors, roof access, asbestos, plant rooms, water systems, electrical defects, contractor activity and public routes all create risks that can change quickly.

A risk assessment framework gives consistency. It prevents decisions being based only on habit, pressure or who shouts loudest. It also gives an auditor, insurer, enforcing authority or interview panel a clear evidence trail.

## Core Principles

- Risk assessment is a management tool, not a paperwork exercise.
- Assess the real task, real location and real people exposed.
- Use competent input where technical judgement is needed.
- Apply the hierarchy of control: eliminate, substitute, engineer, administer, PPE.
- Record significant findings clearly.
- Assign owners and deadlines for additional controls.
- Review assessments when something changes, not only once a year.
- Keep temporary controls visible until permanent controls are complete.
- Escalate intolerable or blocked risks with evidence.

## Step-by-Step Process

1. Define the activity, location or hazard being assessed.
2. Identify who could be harmed, including employees, contractors, tenants, visitors, children, disabled users and vulnerable people.
3. Identify foreseeable hazards and failure points.
4. Record existing controls and check whether they actually work.
5. Rate the residual risk using a consistent likelihood and consequence method.
6. Decide additional controls using the hierarchy of control.
7. Assign each action to a named owner with a due date.
8. Communicate the controls to affected people.
9. Store evidence with the assessment.
10. Review after incidents, changes, defects, complaints, new information or at the planned review date.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- maintain a live FM risk register;
- ensure significant FM activities have current risk assessments;
- link high-risk defects to risk assessments and action trackers;
- require contractors to submit task-specific RAMS for non-routine or higher-risk work;
- use permits for work that needs formal control;
- include visitor and public safety in assessments for attractions, events and open estates;
- check that actions from assessments are completed;
- brief site teams and contractors on relevant controls;
- review recurring incidents and near misses for weak controls;
- report unresolved high risks to senior management.

## Evidence to Keep

- Risk assessments.
- Risk register.
- Safe systems of work.
- RAMS.
- Permits to work.
- Photographs of hazards and controls.
- Inspection records.
- Training and briefing records.
- Incident and near-miss reports.
- Action tracker with owners and completion evidence.
- Competent advice or survey reports where needed.
- Review records showing what changed and why.

## Review Frequency

- Baseline review: at least annually for live FM risk assessments.
- Immediate review: after an incident, near miss, enforcement concern, control failure or significant defect.
- Change review: before changes to layout, occupancy, equipment, process, event use, contractor scope or building condition.
- Project review: before work starts and whenever scope or conditions change.
- Risk register review: monthly in active operational environments.

## Red Flags

- Assessments copied between sites without local checks.
- No named owner for high-risk actions.
- Assessment says "low risk" but evidence shows repeated incidents.
- Temporary controls stay in place for months.
- Contractor RAMS do not match the actual task.
- No review after an incident or near miss.
- Public-facing risks are assessed only from an employee viewpoint.
- Risk ratings are changed to avoid escalation.

## Longleat / Visitor Attraction Relevance

A visitor attraction adds public safety, animal operations, heritage buildings, events, traffic, weather and high-footfall pressures. A risk assessment must consider guests who do not know the site, children, queues, mobility limitations, crowd behaviour and emergency access.

Example FM risk areas include estate roads, visitor paths, animal enclosure infrastructure, temporary attractions, roof work above public areas, contractor activity in live zones and emergency generator failure during peak attendance.

## Example Scenario

A visitor walkway has uneven surfacing after heavy rain. One near miss is reported during a busy weekend.

The FM response is to inspect the route, photograph the defect, assess footfall and user vulnerability, apply temporary controls such as barriers or a diversion, log a permanent repair, assign ownership, set a due date and review whether the route needs more frequent inspection during bad weather.
