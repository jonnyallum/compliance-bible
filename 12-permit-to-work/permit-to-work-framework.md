# Permit-to-Work Framework

## Plain English Summary

A permit to work is a formal written control for higher-risk work. It confirms the task, hazards, precautions, authorisation, time limits, handback and evidence before the work starts.

In FM, permits are commonly used for hot works, roof access, electrical isolation, fire alarm isolation, sprinkler impairment, confined spaces, asbestos-related controls, excavation, lifting operations and intrusive works.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Permits prevent dangerous assumptions. They make sure the right people have agreed the task, isolations, access, emergency arrangements, public segregation and handback.

In a live FM environment, uncontrolled work can disable fire systems, expose asbestos, cause power loss, create fire risk, block escape routes or place contractors and visitors in danger.

## Core Principles

- A permit is an authorisation control, not a replacement for RAMS.
- Permits must be task-specific, time-limited and location-specific.
- The issuer must understand the hazard and have authority to approve.
- The acceptor must understand and agree the controls.
- Site conditions must be checked before issue.
- Changes in scope, time or conditions require review or reissue.
- Work must be handed back and closed formally.
- Emergency arrangements and communication must be clear.
- Permit records must be auditable.

## Step-by-Step Process

1. Decide whether the task requires a permit using the permit matrix.
2. Confirm RAMS, competence and supporting documents are suitable.
3. Visit or verify the work area before issue.
4. Identify isolations, access controls, fire precautions, rescue arrangements and affected parties.
5. Issue the permit with clear start/end time, location, task and controls.
6. Obtain contractor acceptance before work starts.
7. Display or make the permit available at the work location.
8. Monitor the work while the permit is live.
9. Suspend or cancel the permit if conditions change.
10. Close the permit only after inspection, handback and evidence are complete.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- define which activities require permits;
- train permit issuers and acceptors;
- keep permit templates simple but complete;
- check RAMS before issuing permits;
- ensure fire system impairments and isolations are communicated;
- coordinate permits where multiple contractors may interact;
- require fire watch and post-work checks for hot works;
- require rescue plans for confined space, MEWP or fall-arrest work;
- prevent remote approval where a site check is needed;
- audit closed permits for quality and completion.

## Evidence to Keep

- Permit request.
- Approved RAMS.
- Competence records.
- Isolation certificates or lock-off evidence.
- Fire system impairment records.
- Hot works fire watch records.
- Gas testing records where applicable.
- Rescue plans where applicable.
- Photographs of controls or handback.
- Permit close-out and handback record.
- Incident or stop-work records.
- Permit audit findings.

## Review Frequency

- Permit requirement matrix: at least annually and after incidents or near misses.
- Permit forms: annually or after audit findings.
- Issuer competence: before authorisation and periodically.
- Live permits: monitored during the work at a risk-based frequency.
- Closed permits: sample audit monthly in active sites.
- Hot works: check during work and after completion for the stated fire-watch period.

## Red Flags

- Permit issued without task-specific RAMS.
- Permit copied from a previous job without site review.
- Work starts before permit acceptance.
- Permit remains open after work has finished.
- Scope changes but the permit is not reissued.
- Fire alarm or sprinkler isolation not communicated.
- Hot works have no fire watch or post-work inspection.
- Permit issuer has no authority or competence.
- Digital permit system allows approval without location control.

## Longleat / Visitor Attraction Relevance

In a visitor attraction, permits must protect the public as well as workers. Hot works near heritage buildings, roof repairs above visitor areas, electrical isolation affecting ticketing or animal systems, and intrusive works near enclosure infrastructure all need extra coordination.

Permit planning should include guest routes, animal welfare interfaces, emergency access, event schedules, weather, communications teams and out-of-hours handback.

## Example Scenario

A contractor needs to cut and weld a bracket in a plant room beside stored packaging. The job is urgent because a pump is vibrating.

The FM response is to remove combustible materials, review hot works RAMS, confirm fire extinguishers and fire watch, check whether fire detection needs isolation, issue a time-limited hot works permit, monitor the work and close the permit only after post-work fire checks.
