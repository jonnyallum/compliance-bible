# Contractor Management Framework

## Plain English Summary

Contractor management is the system for selecting competent contractors, agreeing safe work, controlling site access, monitoring performance and keeping evidence that the work was managed properly.

For FM, this covers planned maintenance, reactive repairs, projects, specialist statutory work, cleaning, grounds, waste, water hygiene, electrical work, asbestos, fire safety and lifting equipment.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Contractors often perform the highest-risk FM tasks. They may work at height, isolate services, disturb building fabric, use chemicals, enter plant rooms, create hot works, operate lifting equipment or work near the public.

If contractor control is weak, the client organisation can still be exposed. The key question is whether FM planned the work, chose competent people, shared site information, checked RAMS, controlled access and monitored what actually happened.

## Core Principles

- Select contractors for competence, not only price or speed.
- Define the scope clearly before work starts.
- Share site hazards such as asbestos, fire routes, fragile roofs, public areas and restricted zones.
- Require proportionate RAMS for the work.
- Use permits for hazardous or non-routine tasks.
- Induct contractors before site work.
- Monitor live work, especially where conditions change.
- Stop work where controls are missing or unsafe.
- Review performance after the job.
- Keep records that prove decisions and checks.

## Step-by-Step Process

1. Define the task, risk level, site area and operational constraints.
2. Pre-qualify the contractor using competence, insurance, training and relevant accreditations.
3. Issue site information, including asbestos, fire, access, welfare, emergency and public safety requirements.
4. Request task-specific RAMS and supporting documents.
5. Review RAMS against the actual scope and site hazards.
6. Agree permits, isolations, out-of-hours work or supervision requirements.
7. Complete induction and sign-in before work starts.
8. Monitor the work at a frequency proportionate to risk.
9. Record changes, stop-work decisions and completed permits.
10. Review quality, safety, evidence and lessons learned after completion.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- maintain an approved contractor list;
- check insurance, competence, training and statutory qualifications;
- ensure asbestos information is checked before intrusive works;
- require hot works, roof access, isolation and confined space permits where relevant;
- review RAMS before attendance for planned works;
- refuse generic RAMS that do not match the task;
- brief contractors on live visitor, tenant or operational constraints;
- monitor high-risk works in person or through a competent delegate;
- record contractor non-conformance;
- remove or suspend contractors who repeatedly fail controls.

## Evidence to Keep

- Approved contractor record.
- Insurance certificates.
- Competence certificates and training evidence.
- Scope of work.
- RAMS.
- Permits to work.
- Site induction records.
- Sign-in and sign-out records.
- Asbestos register acknowledgement where relevant.
- Waste carrier licences and transfer notes where relevant.
- Monitoring inspection records.
- Completion certificates, service reports and photographs.
- Contractor performance reviews.

## Review Frequency

- Contractor approval: before first appointment and at least annually for active contractors.
- Insurance: before work starts and before expiry.
- RAMS: before each task and whenever scope or conditions change.
- Induction: before first attendance, with refresh for long-term or returning contractors.
- Live monitoring: proportionate to task risk.
- Performance review: after significant works, incidents, repeated defects or at contract review.

## Red Flags

- Contractor starts before RAMS are reviewed.
- Generic RAMS mention hazards not present and miss site-specific hazards.
- Intrusive work proceeds without asbestos information.
- Contractor signs in but no one knows where they are working.
- Work continues after scope changes without revised controls.
- Hot works occur without permit, fire watch or post-work check.
- Competence evidence is missing or expired.
- Poor performance is tolerated because the contractor is familiar.

## Longleat / Visitor Attraction Relevance

Contractor control is harder in a live visitor attraction because work may happen near guests, animals, heritage structures, estate roads and operational teams. A contractor who is safe in an empty office may create unacceptable risk beside a queue, enclosure, public path or event space.

FM must consider visitor segregation, noise, emergency access, animal welfare interfaces, biosecurity, out-of-hours working and communications with operations teams.

## Example Scenario

A roofing contractor arrives to repair a leak above a visitor cafe. Their RAMS mention ladder use but not public segregation, fragile surfaces, weather limits or rescue arrangements.

The FM response is to stop the start of work, require revised RAMS, confirm roof access controls, agree visitor exclusion zones, check competence, issue any required permit and only allow work once the controls match the real task.
