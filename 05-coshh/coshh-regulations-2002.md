## Plain English Summary

The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 require employers to prevent or adequately control exposure to substances that can harm health.

For FM, COSHH covers cleaning chemicals, maintenance products, fuels, oils, paints, adhesives, dusts, fumes, biological agents and some work processes that create hazardous substances.

## Why It Matters in Facilities Management

Facilities teams buy, store, use and supervise substances across cleaning, grounds, maintenance, waste, pest control, water hygiene and contractor works.

Poor COSHH control can lead to burns, dermatitis, asthma, poisoning, fire risk, incompatible chemical mixing and long-term occupational disease. It is also a common audit weakness because sites may hold safety data sheets but lack real task-based assessments.

## Who Has Duties

- Employers.
- Self-employed people where health and safety law applies.
- Managers who control work involving hazardous substances.
- Facilities Managers procuring, storing or supervising substances.
- Contractors bringing substances onto site.
- Employees who must use controls properly and report defects.

## What the Law Requires

The core requirements include:

- assess risks from hazardous substances before work starts;
- prevent exposure where reasonably practicable;
- adequately control exposure where prevention is not reasonably practicable;
- apply good control practice and workplace exposure limits where relevant;
- maintain, examine and test control measures;
- provide suitable PPE where other controls are not enough;
- monitor exposure and provide health surveillance where required;
- provide information, instruction and training;
- prepare procedures for accidents, incidents and emergencies involving hazardous substances.

## FM Actions

A Facilities Manager should:

- keep a current hazardous substance inventory;
- remove unnecessary or duplicate chemicals;
- obtain current safety data sheets;
- write task-based COSHH assessments, not just product folders;
- check storage, segregation, labelling and spill arrangements;
- confirm dilution systems, ventilation and PPE are suitable;
- ensure cleaners and maintenance staff understand safe use;
- require contractors to declare hazardous substances before work;
- check local exhaust ventilation or other controls where relevant;
- investigate spills, exposure symptoms and repeated unsafe use.

## Evidence to Keep

- Hazardous substance inventory.
- Safety data sheets.
- COSHH risk assessments.
- Training and toolbox talk records.
- PPE issue and replacement records.
- Local exhaust ventilation test records where applicable.
- Exposure monitoring records where applicable.
- Health surveillance records where applicable.
- Spill response records.
- Contractor substance declarations.
- Waste disposal records for hazardous substances.

## Review Frequency

- COSHH assessments: review at least annually, and sooner after substance changes, process changes, incidents, symptoms or new exposure information.
- Substance inventory: review when products are purchased, removed or replaced.
- Safety data sheets: check when products change and periodically for current versions.
- Control measures: inspect routinely and test at statutory or risk-based intervals where applicable.
- Training: refresh when products, tasks or controls change.

## Red Flags

- COSHH folder contains only safety data sheets.
- Unlabelled bottles or decanted chemicals.
- Bleach mixed with other cleaning products.
- Chemicals stored together without segregation.
- Staff using products without training or PPE.
- Contractors bring substances onto site without approval.
- No spill kit or emergency procedure.
- Health complaints such as dermatitis or breathing issues ignored.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I separate product information from risk control. A safety data sheet tells me what the substance is; the COSHH assessment explains how our people use it safely in our building and task.
